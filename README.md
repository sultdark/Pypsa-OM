PyPSA-Oman is an open, Python-based capacity-expansion and dispatch model for analysing Oman’s electricity transition.

The model is derived from the PyPSA-Earth workflow and uses the PyPSA optimisation framework. It combines open geospatial, weather, power-plant, demand, and technology data with country-specific calibration and validation for Oman.

PyPSA-Oman is designed to support transparent analysis of:

electricity generation and demand;

renewable-energy expansion;

transmission reinforcement;

battery and hydrogen-based storage;

direct power-sector CO₂ emissions;

renewable-energy curtailment; and

long-term electricity-system transition scenarios.

The current model represents Oman through a clustered high-voltage AC network and evaluates a validated 2024 base year, a preliminary 2030 transition scenario, and a 2050 zero-direct-emission scenario.

Key Model Features
Spatial representation: 30 clustered AC buses based on the PyPSA-Earth network-building workflow.

Network scope: 132 kV, 220 kV, and 400 kV AC transmission corridors.

Temporal representation: 2,920 weighted three-hourly snapshots, representing 8,760 hours.

Weather year: ERA5-based solar and wind profiles for 2013.

Base-year validation: 2024 fixed-capacity dispatch compared with official Nama PWP electricity statistics.

Investment modelling: solar PV, onshore wind, storage, conversion technologies, and transmission expansion where enabled.

Flexibility options: battery storage, hydrogen storage, electrolysers, fuel cells, and hydrogen transport links.

Scenario outputs: generation, installed capacity, curtailment, storage, transmission reinforcement, and direct CO₂ emissions.

Workflow: PyPSA, PyPSA-Earth, Snakemake, xarray, and Python-based analysis scripts.

Transparency: assumptions, scenario definitions, data sources, validation results, and model limitations are documented explicitly.

Model Overview
PyPSA-Oman uses PyPSA’s linear optimisation framework to minimise annualised investment and operating costs subject to:

nodal electricity balances;

generator and transmission capacity constraints;

renewable-resource availability;

storage state-of-charge equations;

linearised power-flow constraints;

technology-conversion efficiencies; and

scenario-specific direct CO₂ limits.

The model is intended for structural electricity-system analysis. It does not reproduce all substation-level, distribution-level, stability, reserve, or contingency requirements.

Known Limitations
The current version has several limitations:

Temporal resolution: three-hourly snapshots do not represent sub-hourly balancing.

Network formulation: linearised power flow does not capture reactive power, voltage stability, or full AC security.

Operational detail: unit commitment, reserves, forecast error, and N−1 contingency constraints are not represented.

Weather coverage: the current scenarios use one historical weather year.

Demand: electricity demand is exogenous rather than endogenously coupled to electrification, desalination, or export-hydrogen production.

Spatial detail: the 30-bus model is a national planning abstraction and does not reproduce all substations, distribution networks, or the full Musandam system.

Emissions: reported values cover direct generator emissions, not complete life-cycle emissions.

Economic inputs: scenario-year technology costs and annualised cost accounting are still being reviewed.

Uncertainty: the current release reports three scenarios rather than a full sensitivity ensemble.

Engineering interpretation: optimised capacities and reinforcement ratios are planning signals, not construction-ready designs.
